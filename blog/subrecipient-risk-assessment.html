Your Automation Vendor Doesn't Know What a Subrecipient Risk Assessment Actually Requires

If you manage federal awards, you've done this: opened a spreadsheet, pulled up SAM.gov in another tab, navigated to the Federal Audit Clearinghouse in a third, and started piecing together a risk assessment for a subrecipient you've worked with for years. You already know they're fine. But 2 CFR 200.332(c) doesn't care what you know — it cares what you can document.

Every week, another vendor pitches "automated compliance" for grants management. They'll automate your subrecipient monitoring, they say. Streamline risk assessments. Reduce manual effort.

Most of them have never actually performed a subrecipient risk assessment.

That's not a knock on engineers — they build excellent systems. But a subrecipient risk assessment under the Uniform Guidance isn't a data lookup. It's a regulatory judgment call that requires understanding what the regulation actually asks you to evaluate, why each factor matters, and how the results cascade into your monitoring plan, your subaward terms, and ultimately your single audit exposure.

What the Regulation Actually Requires

Section 200.332(c) requires pass-through entities to evaluate each subrecipient's fraud risk and risk of noncompliance to determine appropriate monitoring. The regulation specifies four factors to consider:

1. Prior experience with the same or similar subawards. Not just "have you worked with them before" — whether the subrecipient has managed awards of similar scope, complexity, and regulatory requirements. A community nonprofit that's managed a $50K foundation grant is not automatically ready for a $500K NIH subaward with effort reporting requirements.

2. Results of previous audits. This means more than checking whether they had a Single Audit. It means reviewing whether the same or similar subawards were audited as a major program, whether there were findings, and whether those findings are relevant to your award. The OIG has cited pass-through entities specifically for failing to document this review — not for failing to do it, but for failing to document it.

3. New personnel or substantially changed systems. This is the factor most automation tools ignore entirely. A subrecipient that was low-risk last year may not be low-risk this year if their grants manager left and their accounting system migrated. This requires actual inquiry — you can't pull it from a database.

4. Extent and results of Federal agency monitoring. If your subrecipient also receives direct federal awards, has the awarding agency conducted monitoring visits? What were the results? This information isn't in SAM.gov. It often requires asking the subrecipient directly or reviewing their correspondence with the agency.

Here's the part the automation vendors miss: these four factors don't produce a binary pass/fail. They produce a risk level that determines your entire monitoring approach — the frequency of financial report reviews, whether you require detailed transaction reports, whether you impose specific conditions under §200.208, whether you conduct site visits. Get the risk assessment wrong and your monitoring plan is either wasteful or inadequate. Both are audit findings waiting to happen.

The Real Problem with Automating This

The first two factors — prior experience and audit history — are partially automatable. You can build a system that pulls Single Audit data from the Federal Audit Clearinghouse, checks SAM.gov for exclusions, and flags subrecipients whose audits identified relevant findings. That's useful and worth building.

But factors three and four require human judgment informed by domain expertise. "Substantially changed systems" isn't a field in anyone's database. Neither is "the grants manager who understood your reporting requirements left six months ago and her replacement has never managed an NIH subaward." An automation tool that skips these factors and calls itself a "risk assessment solution" is giving you a false sense of compliance.

The best automation for subrecipient risk assessments doesn't replace the judgment — it structures it. It pre-populates what can be pre-populated, flags what needs human review, enforces documentation, and connects the risk rating to a tiered monitoring plan so the downstream consequences are automatic even when the assessment itself requires thought.

That's the kind of system I build. Not because I'm a better engineer — but because I've sat in the chair, performed these assessments by hand, reviewed the Single Audits, written the management decision letters, and explained to stakeholders why a subrecipient was rated medium instead of high.

The Checklist

Before you buy an automation tool for subrecipient monitoring — or hire someone to build one — use this checklist. Half of it tests whether the system covers the regulatory requirements. The other half tests whether the person building it understands why those requirements exist.

Regulatory Coverage (6 items)
Does the system verify exclusion status in SAM.gov before every subaward, per §200.332(a)?
Does it pull and flag Single Audit results from the Federal Audit Clearinghouse — including whether relevant programs were audited as major programs?
Does it capture prior subaward experience — not just "have we worked with them" but scope, size, and regulatory similarity?
Does it include structured prompts for personnel changes and system changes at the subrecipient — the factor most tools skip?
Does it capture federal agency monitoring activity and results for subrecipients who also receive direct federal awards?
Does the risk rating connect to a tiered monitoring plan with defined consequences (reporting frequency, site visits, specific conditions under §200.208)?
Domain Expertise of the Builder (5 items)
Can your vendor explain the difference between a subrecipient and a contractor under §200.331 — and why it matters for monitoring?
Can they explain what happens when a subrecipient's Single Audit identifies a finding on a program similar to yours — and what your obligation is as pass-through entity?
Do they understand that the $1,000,000 Single Audit threshold (raised from $750K in Oct 2024) means some subrecipients who previously required audits may no longer — and what that means for your monitoring approach?
Can they articulate why a risk assessment isn't a one-time event — and what triggers a reassessment mid-award?
Have they ever had to explain a subrecipient risk rating to stakeholders? To an OIG reviewer?

If the system covers all six regulatory items and the builder can answer all five domain questions, you're probably in good hands. If not, you might be automating a process that produces documented noncompliance faster than you could produce it manually.

The technology isn't the hard part. Understanding what to automate — and what still requires a human who has actually done this work — is.
